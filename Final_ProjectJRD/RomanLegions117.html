Legio I Adiutrix

1st Supporter Legion

  • Emblem: Pegasus.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: 68 CE, to serve new emperor Galba.
  • Recruitment Area: Initially, Gallia Narbonesis and Italy.
  • Postings: Misenum, Spain, Mogontiacum, Sirmium, Brigetio, Dacia, Parthia, Brigetio.
  • Battle Honors: Battle of Old Camp, 70 CE.
    Trajan's Dacian Wars 101-106 CE.
    Trajan's Parthian Campaign, 114-116 CE.
    Marcus Aurelius' German Wars, 161-180 CE.
  • Notable Commander: Publius Helvius Pertinax, future emperor.

Cobbled together with citzens from Vienna and sailors from the Misenum fleet during thew war of seccession, the 1st Adiutrix would fight for the losing side before proving itself under Trajan, ending their illustrious career with Stilicho's desperate defense of Italy from the Visgoths in the fifth-century.

In late 68 CE, Nero raised a new legion, taking the unprecedented step of enlisting sailors from the Roman battle fleet based at Misenum. This was unprecedented because sailors were not citizens (they weren’t slaves either but salaried free men), and recruitment into the Roman Legion had been, up to that point, exclusively from the Roman citizenry. However, Nero, at the age of 30, committed suicide after his guards deserted him, leaving the sailors without an eagle and standards and, more importantly, without the citizenship they were promised outside the gates of a overcrowded Rome. The Senate had already recognized the claim to the throne to 70-year-old Sulpicius Galba, who was the governor of Nearer Spain. As Galba approached Rome he was rushed by the 5,000 sailors who demanded enlistment, they became increasingly vocal about their demands, scaring Galba who order his cavalry to “ride over them”, killing many and incarcerating the rest. The ensuing slaughter and imprisonment of the mostly unarmed sailors was not well received by Romans around the Empire and was considered a bad omen to the start of his reign.

Before Nero committed suicide, the city of Vienna in Narbon Gaul raised a "legions" to defend itself from possible attack by the 1st Italica who were stationed in the neighboring city of Lugdunum. The two cities had a rivalry that stretched back to the first-century BCE and Vienna supported Galba and the other Nero, thus the citizens of Lugdunum tried to persuade the 1st Italica to attack Vienna. The elders of Vienna claimed they were creating a legion to support the 1st Italica, hence the name 1st Adiutrix, or 1st Supporter Legion; literally, the legion in support of the 1st. As Galba passed through the province, Vienna presented the troops they had raised for him, he took them back to Rome and officially commissioned them into service. Galba was killed by a disgruntled legionary of the 15th Apollinaris and the Praetorian Guards hailed Otho, who had marched with Galba to Rome the previous autumn, as emperor and the Senate endorsed the choice. One of his first moves was to release the remaining sailors from captivity and enlisted them into the 1st Adiutrix.

Less than two months after the seaman officially joined the legion, they were sent off into battle against the forces of Vitellius in 69 CE. Ironically, they would face the 1st Italica, they legion it was created to counter, at the First Battle of Bedriacum. Otho’s army lost, the legion surrendered, and Vitellius sent it to Spain. In 70 CE, it was sent to the Mogontiacum on the Rhine by new emperor Vespasian, where it participated in the defeat of the Civilis Revolt. It was removed from the Rhine and sent to Pannonia by Domitian. During the reign of Nerva is was stationed at Brigetio, on the Danube. It would become front and center for both of Trajan’s Dacian Wars, performing well enough that Trajan took it to the East with him for his Parthian campaign. It was sent back to Brigetio, by Hadrian, where it would stay for the next 200 years defending the Danube. The records show it was still in existence early in the fifth-century as part of the army of the Eastern Roman emperor, under the command of the Duke of Valeriae Ripensis.

Legio I Germanica

Germanicus' 1st Legion

  • Other Titles: Augusta; withdrawn 19 BCE.
  • Emblem: Pompey's lion with sword in paw symbol.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: Stemmed from Pompey the Great's most elite legion.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally Italy. Later Spain.
  • Postings: Hispania, Gallia, Colonia Agrippinensis, Bonna.
  • Battle Honors: Cantabrian Wars, 29-19 BCE.
    Tiberius' German campaigns, 15-5 BCE.
    Battle of Idistavisus, 15 CE.
    Battle of Angrivar Barrier, 15 CE.
    Battle of Long Bridges, 15 CE.
    First Battle of Bedriacum, 69 CE.
    Battle of Old Camp, 70 CE.

The decendent of Pompey the Great's most elite legion, it won and then quickly lost the esteemed ‘Augusta’ title during the Cantabrian Wars, but gained a new one, ‘Germanica’, fighting Arminius’ Germans for Germanicus, only to disgrace itself by turning traitor in the Civilis Revolt, earning it’s abolishment.

After valiantly fighting for Pompey during the civil war at all the major battles, Pharsalus, Thapsus, and Munda, it eventually, as all the legions did, end up in the hands of Augustus. It was sent to Spain to fight in the Cantabrian Wars, where it was given the title ‘Augusta’ for it’s valiant service around 25 BCE. Marcus Agrippa stripped the legion of this title for cowardice when the war flared up again in 19 BCE. He then transferred it to Gaul. By 9 CE, it was stationed at what would be Colonia Agrippinensis with the 5th Alaudae, as part of the army of the Lower Rhine (ALR). It participated in a mutiny in 14 CE, before taking part in Germanicus Caesar’s retaliatory campaigns into Germany. In the 15 CE Battle of the Long Bridges it saved the army commander Aulus Caecina as a pivotal point. It was given the honorific ‘Germanica’ as recognition for its duty, the only legion of seven to receive a title.

On the 1st of January 69 CE, the legions of the Rhine were required to take their annual oath to the emperor, who at this point was Galba, but the legions would not have it. It was the commander of the 1st Germanica, Fabius Valens, who led the movement to hail Vitellius, commander of the army of the Upper Rhine (AUR), as emperor. After Galba was assassinated and replaced by Otho, several cohorts of the 1st Germanica marched to Italy with Valens to overthrow the new emperor. They were victorious at the First Battle of Bedriacum and succeeded in overthrowing Otho, but the remainder of the legion was caught up in the Cvilis Revolt and had surrendered to the rebels. Then the cohorts in Italy were defeated by Vespasian’s legions. While the remaining Rhine cohorts of the 1st defected back into Vespasian’s army and helped end the Civilis Revolt at the Battle of Old Camp, Vespasian was so disgusted with the legion’s involvement with the rebels that he disbanded it the same year.

Legio I Italica

1st Italian Legion

  • Informal Title: Phalanx of Alexander.
  • Emblem: Boar.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: 66 CE, by Nero.
  • Recruitment Area: Italy.
  • Postings: Gallia Cisalpina, Lugdunensis, Novae, Dacia, Novae.
  • Battle Honors: First Battle of Bedriacum, 69 CE.
    Dacian Wars, 101-106 CE.
    Marcus Aurelius' German Wars, 167-175 CE.

Raised by Nero in 66 CE for an invasion of Parthia, it was sidelined because of the Jewish Revolt, won it’s first battle but not it’s second, then was sent to the Danube border where it would fight off successive invasions by the Germans, Sarmatians, Goths, and Huns.

Nero created the 1st Italica Legion in 66 CE to be part of one of two massive military operations in the East. The first was a push into Ethiopia from Egypt, and the other, which the 1st was made for, was an invasion of Parthia. The 1st was unique because was the first legion recruited in Italy for a century, and Nero wanted all of the men to be 6 (Roman) feet tall, and equipped in the manner of a Macedonian phalanx; small round shields, spears 4-6 meters (13-20 feet) long, and a tight forward facing formation. Unfortunately for Nero, but likely fortunate for the men of the 1st, the Jewish Revolt of that same year erupted and put those plans on hold. Instead the 1st was sent to Lugdunum in 67 CE to maintain order after the short Vindex Revolt. When Vitellius was hailed as emperor in 69 CE, the legion swore loyalty to Vitellius and marched into Italy to help him with the First Battle of Bedriacum. It was then posted to Novae in Moesia where it would remain, apart from service in Trajan’s Dacian Wars, until the fourth-century CE.

Legio I Minervia

Minerva's 1st Legion

  • Emblem: Gorgon's head, a symbol connected with Minerva and used by Domitian on his armor.
  • Birth Sign: Aries (Ram).
  • Foundation: 82 CE, by Domitian.
  • Recruitment Area: The provinces
  • Postings: Bonna, Moesia, Dacia, Bonna, Syria, Bonna, Lugdunum, Bonna.
  • Battle Honors: Domitian's Chattian Campaign, 83 CE.
    Dacian Wars, 101-106 CE.
    Parthian War, 161-166 CE.
    Marcus Aurelius' Danube Campaign, 167-175 CE.
    Battle of Lugdunum, 197 CE.
  • Notable Commander: Publius Aelius Hadrianus, future emperor.

Created by Domitian for his Chattian War, if fought the Dacians for over 20 year with two emperors before being sent to the East to fight the Parthians with another emperor, it returned to the Rhine and held off invaders till it’s end in early fifth-century.

With a thirst for military glory, Domitian raised a new legion in 82 CE with the intent to attack the Chatti tribe of Germany, a Roman ally. He named this legion after his favorite deity, the goddess Minerva, and stationed it at Bonna on the Rhine, opposite the Chattian homeland. The newly christened 1st Minervia launched the surprise attack the following year and won a swift victory. It was posted to Moesia by Trajan for the purpose of being part of his Dacian Wars, then sent back to Bonna after it’s completion. Marcus Aurelius transferred the legion to the east for his operations against the Parthians in 161 CE, after which it returned to Bonna in 167 CE. They fought for Septimius Severus in the civil wars that broke out after he took the throne in 193 CE, leading Severus forces to victory against Albinus at the Battle of Lugdunum. They stayed as an occupying force for four years, treating the locals harshly for their support of Albinus, Lugdunum never regained it’s former importance. It returned to Bonna where it stayed to stem the flow of invaders until Stilicho recalled it to defend Italy from the Visigoths, in 401 CE. After Stilicho’s death, it appears the legion was destroyed by the Visigoths just before their infamous sack of Rome.

Legio II Adiutrix Pia Fidelis

2nd Supporter 'Loyal and Patriotic' Legion

  • Emblem: Pegasus
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn
  • Foundation: 69 CE. Used by Vitellius, but officially commissioned by Vespasian.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally, Gallia Narbonensis/Italy.
  • Postings: Ravenna, Noviomagus, Lindum, Deva, Singidunum, Aquincum, Dacia, Aquincum, Syria, Aquincum.
  • Battle Honors: Battle of Old Camp, 70 CE.
    Conquest of Brigantia, 71-74 CE.
    Agricola's Welsh Conquest, 82-84 CE.
    Trajan's Dacian Wars, 101-106 CE.
    Marcus Aurelius' Parthian Campaign, 161-166 CE.
  • Notable Second-in-Command: Publius Aelius Hadrianus, future emperor, 95 CE.

Created using the same mismatch of recruits as the 1st Adiutrix for Vitellius, it was used by Vespasian against Civilis, later fighting in Britain for a decade before holding back invaders on the Danube, it would marched on Rome to make Septimius Severus emperor.

While officially becoming a legion on the 7th of March 70 CE by decree of the new emperor Vespasian and at the same time giving it the honorific ‘Pia Fidelis’, the real beginning of the 2nd Adiutrix Pia Fidelis (2nd A-P-F) was much more complicated and irregular than most legions. Just as the 1st Adiutrix before, the bulk of it’s recruits came from the town of Vienna, who, after giving their first legion to Galba, needed to raise another to counter the threat from the town of Lugdunum and the 1st Italica stationed there, once again under the guise that it was to support the 1st Italica, thus the 2nd Adiutrix Legion or 2nd Supporter Legion was born. The Viennase’s fears were well founded, for when Vitellius was hailed emperor and the 1st Italica marched toward Italy to join his army they had to pass Vienna and, inspired by the citizens of Lugdunum, they were eager to loot the city. Vienna was spared a sacking however, when they agreed to pay a small fortune to Manlius Valens, the commander of the 1st Italica, who disrupted 300 sesterces (equal to four months pay) to each legionary. They legion marched on but not before disarming the entire city of Vienna, leaving the city and it’s recruits without weapons.

In the opening campaign between the short two-month war, Otho sent a naval force with several cohorts of the Praetorian Guard to blockade Narbo Gaul. The force, consisting of the Praetorians and marines, came ashore at Forum Julii, a port city with a important strategic position along the entry way into Italy. They were joined by a large group of ‘rustics’(Roman jargon for unsophisticated country people), likely the men that made up the unarmed legion of Vienna, who helped them win two clashes against veteran forces of Vitellius by using slings. However, Otho lost the war and the forces at Forum Julii disbanded, the Praetorians being discharged without pay and the locals returning to their homes. But when Vespasian was hailed emperor by the legions of the East and then shortly after by the legions on the Danube and in the Balkans, a former general of Otho, Suetonius Paulinus, raised a banner for Vespasian at Forum Julii; Paulinus’ home town. The disbanded forces enthusiastically rallied around Paulinus and took over Forum Julii making it the first city in the West to raise a banner for Vespasian. Leaving the ‘rustics’, who had been rearmed properly, to hold the city, Paulinus took the ex-Praetorians over the Alps to link up with the forces from Pannonia.

After Vespasian became emperor, he officially commissioned the 2nd A-P-F and added to it marines from both battle fleets, who had supported him in his bid for the throne, as a reward for their service. The city of Vienna was not honored in any official way, but it was allowed to build a circus for chariot-racing, the building of circus being strictly regulated by the emperors. It’s popularity meant that building one in your city would attract huge crowds, boosting the economy and prestige of the city. As for the 2nd A-P-F, it’s reward was the honorific ‘Pia Fidelis’ and, as a newly minted and full staffed legion, to march against Civilis alongside several well-established and veteran legions. After which it was sent to Britain where it helped the Romans complete the conquest of Wales, and then took part in Agricola’s campaigns which took the legion into Scotland in 84 CE. It was then sent to Singidunum, where it took part in Domitian’s humiliating defeats at the hands of the Dacians and Alemanni. It was then transferred to Aquincum, where it was posted until Marcus Aurelius sent it East with the 1st Minervia to participate in his Parthian Campaigns of 161-166 CE and returned to Aquincum after the war was over. It then joined it’s fellow Pannonian legions in the march on Rome for Septimius Severus, after which it returned to Aquincum where records show it was still located at the beginning of the fifth-century CE.

Legio II Augusta

Augustus' 2nd Legion

  • Emblem: Pegasus.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: In Italy, by Pompey the Great, for service in Spain.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally, Northern Italy.
  • Postings: Nearer Spain, Lower Germany, Argentoratum, Britain, Isca, Dumnoniorum, Glevum, Isca, Carpow, Richborough.
  • Battle Honors: Cantabrian Wars, 29-19 BCE.
    Germanicus' German Campaigns, 14-16 CE.
    Invasion of Britain, 43 CE.
    Conquest of Wales, 80 CE.
  • Notable Commanders: Publius Vitellius, the uncle of the future Emperor Vitellius, 14-16 CE.
    Titus Vespasianus, future emperor, 42-47 CE.

Earning the honorific ‘Augusta’ for it’s service in the Cantabrian Wars, under the commander Vespasian it won more than thirty battles against King Caratacus in Britain, but would disgrace itself during the the Boudicca Revolt, before spending many years stamping out Welsh resistance.

After the civil wars that ended the Roman Republic, the 2nd was sent to Spain to take part in the grueling Cantabrian Wars, where it gained it’s honorific. It was then sent to the Rhine following the disaster that was the Battle of Teutoberg Forest, where Rome lost three legions, in 9 CE to replace fallen legions. It then participated in Germanicus’ campaign into Germania, 14-16 CE, in search of the one who orchestrated the destruction of those fallen legions, Arminius, and the recovery of the lost eagle standards. Germanicus was recalled in 17 CE, and the 2nd was sent to Argentoratum. In 43 CE, it took part in Claudius’ invasion of Britain, as one of four legions. Under command of the future emperor Vespasian, then a praetor, the 2nd Augusta pushed along the Southern coast of England in an impressively efficient advance that overran all opposition, fighting thirty battles, taking twenty towns, and occupying the Isle of Wight.

The 2nd Augusta stopped it’s advance at Isca Dumnoniorum, where it built a 42-acre permanent base which became it’s home for several decades. While the legion was in Britain during the Boudiccan Revolt, it’s camp-prefect infamously ignored orders to march in support of the governor, Suetonus Paulinus; the camp-prefect later committed suicide.In 67 CE, elements of the legion were moved to Glevum, then eight years later the whole legion was moved to Isca, while completely abandoning it’s Exeter base. Between the years 122-136 CE, vexillations participated in the construction fo the famous Hadrian Wall. Then in 290 CE, the 2nd dismantled it’s base at Isca and transferred to Carpow in Scotland, to contend with incursions made by the Picts and Scots. By around 390 CE, the legion was located at Richborough in Kent, but the unit was a shadow of it’s former self. Now only about a tenth it’s original size, it would end it’s days as a small frontier guard unit under the command of the Count of the Saxons Shore.

Legio II Traiana

Trajan's 2nd Legion

  • Emblem: Hercules' hammer and lightning bolt.
  • Birth Sign: Aries.
  • Foundation: By Trajan, c.105 CE.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally, probably German provinces
  • Postings: Laodicea, Nicopolis
  • Battle Honors: Trajan's Parthian Campaign, 111-114 CE.
    Defense of Alexandria, 172-173 CE.

Taking the name of the emperor that raised it, the 2nd Traiana fought for him against the Parthians and took their capital, ending it’s days in Egypt.

In 105 CE, Emperor Trajan levied two legions for his planned operations in the coming years, one for the second invasion of Dacia and the other for his planned invasion of Parthia. The 2nd Traiana was one of those two legions. Given the number two because of the it’s recruiting area on the Rhine; Hercules, patron deity of the legion, was equivalent to the German god of war, Donar. It was quickly sent to the port city of Laodicea where it waited to participate in the invasion of Parthia. During the invasion, which began in 111 CE, the 2nd participated in the taking of the Parthian capital, Ctesiphon. It returned to Syria and then made it’s way down to Nicopolis in Egypt where it remained, possibly contributing vexillations to stomp out the Second Jewish Revolt of 132-135 CE. In 172 CE, Bucoli herdsmen of the Nile Delta rose up in revolt, lead by a Egyptian priest named Isodorus, and defeated the auxiliary force sent to deal with them. They then besieged Alexandria, which was defended by the 2nd Traiana until the siege could be lifted a year later and the revolt was put down. Presumably, the legion sided with Queen Zenobia of Palmyra in 269 CE when she seized Egypt, because there isn’t a record of them fighting her. As of the fifth-century CE, the records show it was one of six legions based in Egypt, under the command of the Duke of Thebes.

Legio III Augusta

Augustus' 3rd Legion

  • Emblem: Lion, on first-century numismatic evidence (Pegaus also suggested but no proven.)
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: Rasied by Octavian.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally, Cisalpine Gaul. Later, North Africa.
  • Postings: Africa, Ammaedra, Tebessa, Lambaesis.
  • Battle Honors: Tacfarinas' Revolt, 17-23 CE.
    Battle of Carthage, 238 CE.
  • Disbanded: 238 CE. Reformed 253 CE.
  • Notable Commander: Marcus Aurelius Probus, future emperor, 276-282 CE.

Earning it’s keep by putting down the long-lasting Tacfarinas’ Revolt in Tunisia for Emperor Tiberius, it was disbanded for it’s loyalty to the Emperor Maximinus only to be reformed 15 years later, for hundreds of years they were the sole legion in North Africa.

After the civil wars that made Octavian an emperor, he changed his named to Augustus in 29 BCE until he died in 14 CE. Sometime between then the 3rd was given his honorific, it is suggested, with some merit, that it was given the title after the governor, Cornelius Balbus, after a series of successful campaigns against desert tribes in 19 BCE, the same time three other legions gained the same title, except for their roles in the Cantabrian Wars. During this time, the legion was also fighting against the rebels led by Tacfarinas, who’s revolt they finally put down in 23 CE. In 75 CE, the 3rd was transferred to Teresa from their base at Lambaesis, where, in addition to building a new base, it built a town which it continued to work on for another half-century.

In 238 CE, leading citizens of the province of Africa rebelled against the Emperor Maximinus, declaring Gordian I and Gordian II co-emperors. However, the 3rd Augusta remained loyal to the emperor and crushed the usurpers’ army of raw levies outside Carthage, and in the ensuing slaughter Gordian II was killed, which caused Gordian I to commit suicide out of sadness. But the Senate despised Maximinus, and they declared two of their members, Pupienus Maximus and Balbinus, as co-emperors. In response, Maximinus marched his legions from Pannonia and besieged the pro-Senate forces at Aquileia, unfortunately for him, his own troops murdered him in his camp. The Praetorian Guard then murder Pupienus and Balbinus, declaring Gordian I’s 13-year-old grandson, Gordian III, to become the next emperor, and because of the 3rd Augusta was responsible for death of Gordian III’s grandfather and uncle, it was disbanded and the men distributed to other units around the empire.

Fifteen years later, in 253 CE, a year of three emperors, the 3rd Augusta was reformed by Valerian, with Sattonius Jucundus as chief centurion, Sattonius having previously served with the 3rd before it’s disbanding. With Africa in a peaceful state, it sent vexillations to Europe frequently, until it was withdraw from Africa and numbered among the comitatense legions under the command of the Master of Foot. It’s men were then recruited in Gaul, and it remained in Europe until it was sent back by Stilicho to Africa to put down Africa’s rebel governor, Gildo, in 395 CE.

Legio III Cyrenaica

3rd Cyrenaican Legion

  • Emblem: Ammon/Jupiter.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: Mark Anthony, c. 36 BCE.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally, province of Cyrenaica
  • Postings: Egypt, Judea, Bostra, Judea, Bostra.
  • Battle Honors: Siege of Jerusalem, 70 CE.
    Trajan's Parthian Campaign, 114-116 CE.
    Second Jewish Revolt, 132-135 CE.

The resident legion of Egypt for many years, Trajan used it to occupy and annex the new province of Arabia, serving valiantly in Trajan’s Parthian campaigns, it put helped put down the Second Jewish War, banning all Jews from within sight of Jerusalem.

After becoming one of Augustus’ twenty-eight standing legions, it was posted to Egypt. The men of the 3rd were from Cyrenaica, and as such known to venerate the god Ammon, similar to the Roman Jupiter but with horns, and as such this was likely their officially recognized emblem. Based in Alexandria, the legion put down Jewish rioting in that city in the first-century CE and contributed cohorts to Titus for his 70 CE siege of Jerusalem. In 106 CE, it left it’s base of Alexandria to join a task force led by the governor of Syria, Aulus Cornelius Palma, to invade the old kingdom of Nabataea. On the orders of Trajan, Palma created the Roman province Arabia Petraea out of the new conquest and the 3rd built a base at Bostra. The legion was used by Trajan during his Parthian campaigns, after which it returned to Bostra. It then took part in Julius Severus’s slow, grueling operation to take Judea back from the Jewish rebels. It returned to Bostra after putting down the Jewish rebels, where it would remain for the next few hundred years, regularly fighting Persians with mixed results. At the end of the fourth-century CE, records show that the 3rd was still in Arabia, joined by the 4th Martia Legion, a relatively new creation, plus twelve mounted units and five auxiliary cohorts

Legio III Gallica

3rd Gallica Legion

  • Emblem: Three Bulls.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: Reformed in 49 BCE; based on Pompey's 3rd Legion.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally, Gaul. Under Mark Anthony, changed to Syria.
  • Postings: Emesa, Apamea, Cappadocia, Armenia, Judea, Moesia, Rome/Capua, Raphanaea, Judea, Danaba
  • Battle Honors: Corbulo's First Armenian Campaign, 58-60 CE.
    Corbulo's Second Armenian Campaign, 62 CE.
    First Jewish Revolt, 66-67 CE.
    Defeat of the Roxolani Sarmatians, 68 CE.
    Second Battle of Bedriacum, 69 CE.
    Battle of Cremona, 69 CE.
    Battle of Rome, 69 CE.
    Second Jewish Revolt, 132-135 CE.

Losing three cohorts at the outset of the First Jewish Revolt, it was badly mauled in subsequent clashes before they were transferred to Moesia where they single-handedly eliminated 9,000 Sarmatian cavalry, this feared legion the led the way into Italy to defeat Vitellius and make it’s former general, Vespasian, emperor.

After the civil war, Octavian sent the sun-worshiping Syrians, who worshiped the Eastern sun god of Baal also known as Elagabalus, of the 3rd Gallica to Judea. In 58 CE, they sent a vexillation of six cohorts to Cappadocia to take part in Corbulo’s first quick campaign into Armenia, four years later another vexillation participated in Corbulo’s second Armenian campaign. When the First Jewish Revolt erupted in Judea in 66 CE, three of the 3rd’s cohorts were caught by surprise and were wiped out, and the legions remaining cohorts led Vespasian’s 67 CE counter-offensive into Galilee. Despite, or perhaps because of their revenge fueled enthusiasm, the legion suffered so many casualties that the Palatium transferred them to Moesia on the Danube to recoup their losses, where which they arrived in 68 CE.

In the winter of 69 CE, legion’s legate learned that 9,000 Roxolani, a tribe of the Sarmatians, had crossed the frozen Danube. Without hesitating the depleted legion marched out to meet them and in a surprise attack against the Roxolani camp, the 3rd slaughtered them to a man, with few loses of their own. In the summer of that same year, the 3rd learned that their former commander, Vespasian, had been hailed as emperor by the legions of the East. The legion quickly swore fro Vespasian and managed to convince the legions of Moesia, Pannonia, and Dalmatia to do the same. They then led the way into Italy and where at the forefront in the Second Battle of Bedriacum and the Battle of Cremona. At Cremona, all authors agree that a single legionary of the 3rd, Gaius Volusius, entered the camp first, fighting his way though the camp to the rampart, where he waved his hand and shouted that the camp had been taken. They then took Rome and looted the homes of Vitellius supporters and were sent to Capua, where they did the same.

The 3rd Gallica was sent back to the East in 70 CE, to the remote settlement, Raphanaea in Syria. The 3rd participated in the Roman counter-offensive against the Jewish rebels in the Second Jewish Revolt of 132-135 CE. After it was returned to Raphanaea during the reign of Marcus Aurelius until 273 CE when it part of Emperor Aurelian’s campaign to take the East back from Queen Zenobia of Palmyra. During the reign of Diocletian, the 3rd was based at Danaba (modern-day Al-Hafar, Syria). By the late fourth-century CE, the legion was still in Danaba along with the 1st Illyricorum Legion, a unit created in the late third-century CE.

Legio IIII Macedonica

4th Macedonian Legion

  • Emblem: Bull.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: By Pompey the Great.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally Spain or Italy.
  • Postings: Juliobriga, Mogontiacum.

After earning fame during the civil war at the Battle of Philippi, it was posted to a quiet part of Spain before being transferred to the Rhine to replace another legion, there it disgraced itself in Civilis Revolt and was disbanded and reformed as the 4th Flavia.

Legio XII Fulminata

Thundering 12th Legion

  • Emblem: Jupiter's thunderbolt.
  • Birth Sign: Capricorn.
  • Foundation: 58 BCE, by Julius Caesar.
  • Recruitment Area: Originally Cisalpine Gaul.
  • Postings: Egypt, Syria, Raphanaea, Laodicea, Jerusalem, Judea, Melitene, the Danube, Melitene.
  • Battle Honors: Invasion of Ethiopia, 22 BCE.
    Seige of Jerusalem, 70 CE.
    Trajan's Eastern campaign, 114-116 CE.
    Defeat of the Alans, 135 CE.
    Defeat of the Quadi, 174 CE.

Disgraced in the disastrous Armenian campaign of Paetus, and again in the First Jewish Revolt, it proved itself when given a third chance however, then lived up to their name fighting for Marcus Aurelius by winning a battle in a thunderstorm.

The 12th Legion was sent to Egypt at the conclusion of the civil war, taking part in the invasion of Ethiopia in 22 BCE. It was moved to Raphanaea is Syria in 14 CE, to be stationed with the 6th Ferrata, where it stayed until called up for the 62 CE campaign into Armenia by Paetus. During the campaign, which was hastily planned and executed, the legion shamed itself when it became surrounded in it’s fort and refused to fight it’s way out. It retreated back to Syria, without most of it’s supplies or siege equipment. There the legion stayed for four years, until being called up by Gallus for his march on Jerusalem to put down in the First Jewish Revolt. They were turned back, and during the retreat their eagle standard was taken by the rebels, once again the 12th Legion had disgraced itself. Vespasian refused to use this legion for his planned operation into Judea because of it’s poor performance but with his march to Rome, Titus took the reins and was quick to use the 12th for his siege of Jerusalem. Eager to regain their lost valor, they performed well during the siege, so well in fact that Vespasian posted the legion to the newly annexed Cappadocia in 71 CE, becoming the province’s home legion, at their new base at Melitene.

The 12th’s home base remained at Melitene for the next 300 years, from there it regained it’s reputation. In 135 CE, it was part of Arrian’s campaign against the invading Alans in Lesser Armenia, there the legionaries helped Arrian turn back 10,000+ all armored cavalry force. Several decades later, it was transferred to the Danube for Marcus Aurelius’ Germanic wars, helping to fight back the Alemanni and the Quadi German incursions. The King of the Quadi, Ariogaesus, rejoined the war in 174 CE, leading a surprise attack across the Danube. Marcus Aurelius, with his body guard, took the 12th legion and it’s auxiliaries out to meet the Quadi. In this famous battle, the 12th officially earned their title and emblem, when they fought to victory under a fierce lightening storm while outnumbered and surrounded. Records show that the legion went back to Melitene and that by the late fourth-century CE was moved to Armenia along with the 15th Apollinaris, but reduced to border guard units.

Always on the forefront, the mighty 13th fought in Germany for Germanicus, was on the losing and then the winning side of the 69 CE war of succession, and fiercely stormed the Dacian capital in the final stage of the Second Dacian War.

After the civil war, Octavian combined two legions, consisting of one or possibly both 13th Legions, one being Octavian's and the other was Anthony’s. Those the 13th Gemina was born, with recorded lineage dating back to 205 BCE, which was rare for most legions, the 13th began it’s career fighting against Germans one both sides of the Rhine river. It was then to take part in the 15 BCE operation that made Raetia a province. From there it was in a perfect position to react to the Pannonian Revolt in 6 CE and fight a hard campaign to regain control of the provinces, just in time top be quickly transferred to defend the Rhine in the wake of the Virus disaster in 9 CE. It took part in Germanicus’ operations of revenge against the Germans between 14-16 CE, before being transferred to Poetovio in 46 CE. It marched for Otho in 69 CE, where it dishonored itself during the First Battle of Bedriacum, with both sides despising the legion because of it’s poor performance. They were then put to work by Vitellius to build wooden amphitheaters at Cremona and Bononia, where Vitellius and his generals would celebrate their victory, and was then sent back to Poetovio.

When Vespasian was hailed as emperor later that year, the 13th was quick to join the forces on their march into Italy, and with the dishonor hanging over their head, they fought valiantly in the Second Battle of Bedriacum, Battle of Cremona, and Battle of Rome.The 13th was sent back to Poetovio where it remained until 97 CE when it was transferred to Vindonissa. It was called up to take part in both of Trajan’s Dacian Wars, and once Dacia was conquered it set up a base at Apulum in the newly conquered province. From it’s new base it would continually fight against multiple incursions by tribes from the German, Sarmatian, and Gothic people. Emperor Aurelian abandoned the province in 274 CE, finding the province too vulnerable and lacking natural defenses, in favor of the much more defendable Danube river, The legion moved to a new Moesian base at Ratiara, where it remained for the rest of it’s career. Records show that as of 395 CE, the legion had been split apart; part was still based at Ratiara and the other in Egypt.
